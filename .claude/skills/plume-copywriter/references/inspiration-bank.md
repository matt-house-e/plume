# Inspiration Bank — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Curated examples of outstanding website copy organized by page type. For each example: what makes it great and what technique it demonstrates. These are references for quality, not templates to copy. Study the WHY behind the copy, then apply the principle to each unique project.

## Best Homepage Copy

### Stripe (stripe.com)

**Hero:** "Financial infrastructure for the internet"
- **Why it works:** 5 words. Says exactly what Stripe does at the highest level. No jargon. No "leading provider of." The simplicity signals confidence.
- **Technique:** Definitional positioning — claiming an entire category rather than describing a feature.

### Basecamp (basecamp.com)

**Hero:** "Project management software that makes your work life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive."
- **Why it works:** Addresses three real desires (simpler, pleasant, productive). "Work life" is specific to the context. No buzzwords.
- **Technique:** Benefit triplet — three concrete outcomes in one headline.

### Linear (linear.app)

**Hero:** "Linear is a purpose-built tool for planning and building products."
- **Why it works:** "Purpose-built" signals intentionality vs. bloated competitors. Direct, no embellishment.
- **Technique:** Positioning against implied alternatives (general-purpose tools) without naming them.

### Notion (notion.so)

**Hero:** "Your wiki, docs, & projects. Together."
- **Why it works:** Names three specific things the audience already uses separately, then promises to unify them. The period after "Together" adds weight.
- **Technique:** Aggregation value proposition — combining separate tools into one.

### Vercel (vercel.com)

**Hero:** "Your complete platform for the web."
- **Why it works:** "Complete" does the work of a feature page in one word. "For the web" is the audience filter.
- **Technique:** Category ownership — claiming a whole space concisely.

### ConvertKit (convertkit.com)

**Hero:** "The creator marketing platform that helps you reach your audience and make a living."
- **Why it works:** "Creator" is the audience. "Marketing platform" is the category. "Make a living" is the aspiration — not "monetize your content" but the real human goal.
- **Technique:** Aspiration framing — naming what the customer actually wants, not the business outcome.

## Best About Page Copy

### Basecamp — "About Basecamp"

- Opens with what they believe, not what they sell: "We've been doing this since 2004."
- Uses specific numbers: employee count, customer count, years in business
- Deliberately anti-corporate: "We don't have a mission statement, a set of values, or a vision."
- **Technique:** Contrarian positioning — defining themselves by what they're NOT.

### Patagonia — "Our Reason for Being"

- "Build the best product, cause no unnecessary harm, use business to inspire and implement solutions to the environmental crisis."
- The value statement IS the about page content
- Every section connects back to environmental mission
- **Technique:** Mission-led storytelling — the mission IS the differentiator, not an add-on.

### Mailchimp — "About"

- Combines humor with substance
- Team section feels personal, not corporate
- Specific history timeline with real milestones, not generic "founded in..."
- **Technique:** Humanization — showing the real people and culture behind the product.

## Best Landing Page Copy

### Copyhackers (copyhackers.com) — Various Courses

- **Hero pattern:** Direct address + specific outcome + proof
- Uses voice-of-customer language throughout ("I was writing garbage copy")
- Testimonials with extremely specific results ("My sales page conversion rate went from 1.2% to 6.7%")
- Long-form sales copy with clear section breaks and multiple CTA placements
- **Technique:** Customer-voiced copy — using the audience's actual words, captured from surveys and interviews.

### Unbounce — Landing Page Builder

- Meta-effectiveness: their landing page about landing pages converts well
- "Build, publish, and A/B test landing pages without IT"
- Clear problem → solution → proof → CTA structure
- **Technique:** Objection elimination — "without IT" handles the #1 objection in 2 words.

## Best SaaS Copy (Full-Site)

### Ahrefs (ahrefs.com)

- Homepage: "Everything you need to rank higher & get more traffic."
- Uses its own data as proof — shows actual search traffic numbers
- Feature pages lead with the problem each feature solves, not the feature itself
- Blog is a masterclass in SEO content that's genuinely useful
- **Technique:** Product-as-proof — demonstrating the product by using it on themselves.

### Superhuman (superhuman.com)

- "The fastest email experience ever made"
- Deliberately exclusive positioning ("by invitation only" during early growth)
- Every feature description ties back to speed
- Minimal copy — lets the product demonstration do the work
- **Technique:** Single-attribute positioning — owning one word ("fastest") and reinforcing it everywhere.

### Loom (loom.com)

- "Video messaging for work"
- Extremely simple positioning — 4 words covers it
- Feature copy uses specific time savings: "Record your screen in seconds"
- Social proof: "Used by 14M+ people across 200K+ companies"
- **Technique:** Simplicity as brand — the copy mirrors the product's promise of simplicity.

## Best Microcopy

### Mailchimp

- Form validation messages with personality: "Oops! That doesn't look like an email address."
- Encouraging empty states: "This is where your campaigns will live."
- High-five monkey after sending a campaign — copy reinforces the celebration
- **Technique:** Brand personality in unexpected places — microcopy as brand experience.

### Slack

- Loading screen messages that are playful: "Herding cats..."
- Empty channels: "This is the very beginning of the #general channel."
- Error messages are helpful, not technical: "We couldn't post that message. Want to try again?"
- **Technique:** Conversational microinteractions — making functional moments feel human.

### GitHub

- 404 page: "This is not the web page you are looking for" (Star Wars reference)
- Commit messages, pull request templates, and issue descriptions are all examples of excellent technical microcopy
- Empty state guidance: "Create your first repository to get started."
- **Technique:** Audience-appropriate personality — developer humor in developer context.

## Best Case Study Copy

### Shopify — Partner Case Studies

- Lead with the merchant's story, not Shopify's features
- Specific revenue and growth numbers: "Revenue grew 76% year over year"
- Photography of real people and real products
- **Technique:** Merchant-as-hero storytelling — Shopify positions itself as the enabler, not the protagonist.

### HubSpot — Case Studies

- Standardized metric format: clear before/after tables at the top
- Mix of story and data
- Every case study ends with a relevant CTA
- Organized by industry, company size, and challenge — easy for prospects to find "someone like me"
- **Technique:** Categorized proof — making case studies findable by prospect self-identification.

## Best Pricing Page Copy

### Basecamp (basecamp.com/pricing)

- Single plan. One price. "Basecamp costs $99/month, flat."
- Eliminates decision fatigue entirely
- Lists everything included, with specific numbers: "500 GB of storage"
- FAQ addresses "What about..." questions directly
- **Technique:** Radical simplicity — one plan eliminates the entire pricing comparison anxiety.

### Linear (linear.app/pricing)

- Free tier with generous limits, paid tier with clear additional value
- "Built for every team" — the tier names signal audience fit
- Minimal copy per plan — trust the feature list to do the work
- **Technique:** Value demonstration through free tier — let people experience before committing.

## Copy Patterns Worth Studying

### The "What We're Not" Pattern

Basecamp and Hey.com use this repeatedly — defining the brand by what it rejects:
- "We don't do X" (competitive differentiation)
- "You won't find Y here" (expectation management)
- "We believe Z" (value signaling by contrast)

### The Specific Number Pattern

Stripe, Ahrefs, and Superhuman use precise numbers throughout their copy:
- "Millions of businesses" vs. "Stripe processes payments for millions of businesses in 120+ countries"
- Specificity builds credibility without requiring external proof

### The Voice-of-Customer Pattern

Copyhackers and ConvertKit use customer language extensively — pulled from surveys, interviews, and support tickets. The copy literally sounds like the audience because it uses their words.
