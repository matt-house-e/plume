# Case Studies — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Case studies are the most powerful conversion tool on a business website. They combine narrative (which builds emotional connection) with data (which satisfies analytical buyers). A well-written case study answers the prospect's real question: "Can you do for me what you did for them?" This file covers structure, narrative technique, metric presentation, and the interview process for gathering case study material.

## Case Study Structures

### 1. The Classic: Problem → Process → Outcome

The most common and reliable structure. Works for any industry.

| Section | Word Count | Job | Key Question It Answers |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Title** | 10-20 words | Hook with the result | "Is this relevant to me?" |
| **Snapshot** | 30-50 words | Quick summary with key metric | "Should I keep reading?" |
| **Client context** | 40-80 words | Who the client is and their situation | "Are they like me?" |
| **The problem** | 80-150 words | The challenge they faced | "Do I have this problem too?" |
| **The process** | 100-200 words | What you did and how | "How would you approach my problem?" |
| **The outcome** | 80-150 words | Results with metrics | "What results can I expect?" |
| **Client quote** | 30-60 words | Third-party validation | "Do they recommend you?" |
| **CTA** | 15-30 words | Next step | "How do I get started?" |

**Total: 400-750 words** for a standard case study. Go longer (1,000-1,500) only when the story warrants it.

### 2. The Metric-Led Structure

For data-driven audiences (SaaS, B2B, performance marketing). Lead with the numbers.

```
## [Client Name]: [Primary Metric] in [Timeframe]

### At a Glance
| Metric | Before | After | Change |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| [KPI 1] | [X] | [Y] | [+Z%] |
| [KPI 2] | [X] | [Y] | [+Z%] |
| [KPI 3] | [X] | [Y] | [+Z%] |

### The Challenge
[2-3 paragraphs]

### The Approach
[2-3 paragraphs with specific tactics]

### The Results
[Expanded metrics with context]

### In Their Words
[Client testimonial]
```

### 3. The Narrative Structure

For creative agencies, consultancies, and services where the process is the differentiator. Reads more like a story.

```
## [Evocative Title — Not Just "Client Name Case Study"]

[Opening scene — drop the reader into a moment]

[Context — who the client is and what they were facing]

[The turning point — when they decided to act]

[The work — what happened, with specific details]

[The outcome — what changed, with metrics woven into the narrative]

[The reflection — client quote about the experience]
```

## Case Study Title Formulas

| Formula | Example | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **How [Client] [Achieved Result]** | "How Notion Increased Trial-to-Paid by 34%" | Direct, result-focused |
| **[Client]: [Metric] in [Timeframe]** | "Acme Corp: 3x Revenue in 6 Months" | Data-driven audiences |
| **From [Before State] to [After State]** | "From 2% to 11% Conversion Rate" | Before/after transformation |
| **[Verb]-ing [Outcome] for [Client]** | "Rebuilding Retention for a Series B Fintech" | Process-focused agencies |
| **"[Client Quote]"** | "We finally stopped losing customers to our checkout page." | Testimonial-led, relatable |

**Title anti-patterns:** Avoid generic titles like "Client Case Study" or "Success Story." These convey zero information and get zero clicks.

## Presenting Metrics

### Making Numbers Tangible

Raw numbers need context to be meaningful:

| Raw Metric | Better (With Context) |
|---|---|
| "Revenue increased 47%" | "Revenue increased 47% — from $340K to $500K annually" |
| "Load time improved by 3 seconds" | "Load time dropped from 8s to 2s — faster than 95% of competitor sites" |
| "Customer satisfaction at 4.8/5" | "Customer satisfaction jumped from 3.2 to 4.8/5 — highest in the company's history" |
| "Saved 120 hours per month" | "Saved 120 hours per month — the equivalent of a full-time hire" |

### Metric Presentation Rules

1. **Always show the before number.** "47% increase" is weak without knowing the baseline. "From 2% to 11% conversion rate" tells a story.
2. **Use the most impressive framing.** Percentages work for large improvements ("+340%"). Absolute numbers work for tangible outcomes ("$2.3M in new revenue"). Choose the frame that communicates the most.
3. **Round strategically.** Use exact numbers for credibility ("847 new customers" not "about 850"). Round only when the exact number would be confusing.
4. **Add timeframes.** "In 90 days" or "within the first quarter" anchors the result in time and signals speed.
5. **Compare to benchmarks.** "5.2% email open rate" means nothing to most readers. "5.2x the industry average open rate" means something.

### The Before/After Table

The single most scannable way to present results:

```markdown
| Metric | Before | After | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conversion rate | 1.8% | 4.7% | +161% |
| Monthly revenue | $28K | $67K | +139% |
| Bounce rate | 73% | 41% | -44% |
| Avg. session duration | 0:47 | 2:34 | +228% |
```

## Narrative Techniques

### The Hero Is the Client, Not You

The most common case study mistake is making the service provider the hero. The client is the hero. You are the guide who helped them succeed (StoryBrand framework — Donald Miller).

| Wrong (You're the Hero) | Right (Client's the Hero) |
|---|---|
| "We redesigned their website..." | "Acme needed a website that could convert..." |
| "Our team developed a strategy..." | "The team at Acme had a clear goal..." |
| "We achieved a 47% increase..." | "Acme saw a 47% increase..." |

### Show, Don't Tell

| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|---|---|
| "The client was frustrated." | "Their sales team was spending 3 hours a day on manual data entry — time that wasn't going to customers." |
| "The results were impressive." | "Within 60 days, demo requests tripled and the sales team stopped cold-calling entirely." |
| "We had a great working relationship." | "'They felt like part of our team. They challenged our assumptions and that's exactly what we needed.' — Sarah, VP Marketing" |

### Tension and Stakes

Good case studies have tension — something at risk, a deadline, a constraint. This is what makes them readable instead of boring:

- "With only 6 weeks before launch..."
- "After two failed agencies..."
- "With a 40% churn rate threatening their Series B..."

## Client Interview Questions

### For Gathering Case Study Material

Ask the client these questions (in conversation, not a formal survey):

**Context:**
1. Tell me about your business in your own words. What do you do?
2. What was the situation before we started working together?
3. What had you tried before? What didn't work?

**The problem:**
4. What was the biggest challenge or frustration you were facing?
5. What was the cost of not solving this problem? (Time, money, stress, opportunity cost)
6. What finally made you decide to take action?

**The experience:**
7. Why did you choose us over alternatives?
8. What was the process like from your perspective?
9. Was there a moment during the project where you thought "this is going to work"?

**The results:**
10. What specific results have you seen? (Ask for numbers)
11. What surprised you most about the outcome?
12. How has this changed things for you or your team day-to-day?

**The quote:**
13. If someone was considering working with us, what would you tell them?
14. Is there anything else you'd want people to know about the experience?

### Turning Answers into Copy

- Use the client's exact words wherever possible — authentic language > polished language
- If a client says something great but rambling, edit for concision while preserving their voice
- Get explicit permission before publishing quotes, metrics, or the company name
- Offer to let the client review the final case study before publishing

## Case Study Page Design Considerations

While this skill writes copy, not design, note these layout principles for the handoff:

- **Pullout metrics** at the top or in a sidebar — scanners look here first
- **Client logo** prominently placed — readers need to know who this is about
- **Images** of the actual work if applicable (screenshots, photos, before/after)
- **CTA** both at the top (sticky) and bottom
- **Related case studies** at the bottom — keep them browsing
