# Cognitive Principles — Reference Guide

## Purpose

This file covers how people actually read, process, and make decisions on the web. These cognitive and behavioral principles are the invisible foundation of effective copy — they explain WHY certain copy patterns work and inform every structural decision from headline placement to pricing page layout.

## How People Read on the Web

### The F-Pattern (Nielsen Norman Group)

Eye-tracking studies by Jakob Nielsen (2006, updated 2017) show most users scan web pages in an F-shaped pattern:

1. **First horizontal sweep:** Users read across the top of the content area (the headline and first line)
2. **Second horizontal sweep:** Users scan a shorter horizontal line further down (first subheading or bold text)
3. **Vertical sweep:** Users scan down the left side of the content, looking for keywords in subheadings and first words of paragraphs

**Copy implications:**
- Front-load important words in headlines, subheadings, and the first sentence of each paragraph
- The first two words of every paragraph, bullet, and heading are disproportionately important
- Don't bury value in the middle or end of long paragraphs
- Place key information in the first 3 lines of content

### The Layer-Cake Pattern (NNGroup, 2017)

A scanning pattern observed with content that uses strong subheadings:
- Users read the subheadings (the "frosting layers")
- They read the body text (the "cake layers") only under subheadings that seem relevant

**Copy implications:**
- Subheadings should tell a complete story on their own
- A reader who ONLY reads subheadings should understand the page's argument
- Each subheading should include enough context to let the reader decide whether to read the section below

### How Much People Actually Read

| Finding | Data Point | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Users read ~20-28% of text on an average web page | 20% (593 words), 28% (111 words) | Nielsen Norman Group, 2008 |
| Adding 100 words to a page results in users reading ~4.4 more words | Low marginal reading rate | NNGroup |
| Users spend an average of 5.59 seconds looking at written content | First visit to a page | Missouri S&T eye-tracking study |
| 55% of pageviews get fewer than 15 seconds of attention | More than half of visitors barely engage | Chartbeat / Time.com analysis |
| Headlines are read by ~80% of visitors, but only 20% read the full body | The 80/20 rule of headlines | David Ogilvy |

**Copy implications:**
- Write for scanners first, readers second
- Put the most important information in the first 20% of the copy
- Use the inverted pyramid: conclusion first, details second, background third
- Make every sentence earn its place — anything that doesn't add value costs you readers

## Decision Psychology

### The Paradox of Choice (Barry Schwartz)

More options = more anxiety = fewer decisions. Sheena Iyengar's jam study (2000): customers were 10x more likely to purchase when shown 6 jams vs. 24.

**Copy implications for pricing pages:**
- 2-3 pricing tiers is optimal. 4+ creates decision fatigue.
- Recommend one tier ("Most Popular") to simplify the decision
- Feature comparison tables should highlight differences, not list every shared feature
- When presenting options, make the recommended one visually dominant

### Decision Fatigue

Making decisions depletes cognitive resources. Later decisions are lower quality (Danziger et al., 2011 — Israeli parole board study).

**Copy implications:**
- Place the most important CTA early on the page (when cognitive resources are highest)
- Reduce unnecessary choices — one primary CTA per page
- Simplify form fields — every additional field is a micro-decision
- Don't ask the reader to "Choose from our wide range of options" — guide them

### Satisficing vs. Maximizing (Herbert Simon)

Most people are satisficers — they choose the first option that meets their criteria, rather than evaluating all options for the optimal choice.

**Copy implications:**
- Make the recommended option obvious and easy to choose
- The "best option for most people" label on pricing pages works because satisficers are looking for it
- Place your strongest plan where the eye lands first

## Loss Aversion

### Kahneman & Tversky's Prospect Theory

Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).

**Copy applications:**

| Gain Frame (Weaker) | Loss Frame (Stronger) | When to Use Each |
|---|---|---|
| "Save $200/month" | "You're losing $200 every month you wait" | Loss frame for urgent situations |
| "Increase conversions by 30%" | "You're leaving 30% of conversions on the table" | Loss frame for pain-aware audiences |
| "Get more leads" | "Stop losing leads to your competition" | Loss frame when there's a clear competitor threat |

**When NOT to use loss framing:**
- When the reader isn't yet aware of the problem (they'll reject the frame)
- For aspirational products (luxury, lifestyle) — gain framing works better here
- When it could come across as manipulative or fear-mongering

### The Cost of Inaction

Make the cost of not acting tangible:
- "Every day without [solution], you're spending [X hours/dollars] on [manual process]"
- "That's [$amount] in [lost revenue / wasted time] per [timeframe]"

## Anchoring Effect

### Price Anchoring (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)

The first number a person sees influences all subsequent numerical judgments.

**Copy applications:**

| Technique | Example |
|---|---|
| Show the higher price first | "Normally $299 → Now $149" |
| Lead with the ROI number | "$50K in savings → Investment: $5K" (10x anchor) |
| Show competitor pricing | "Enterprise CRMs start at $150/user. Ours is $29/user." |
| Stack the value | "$10K in value for $99" — even if "value" is generous |

### Anchoring Beyond Price

- **Timeframes:** "What used to take 3 weeks now takes 3 hours" (3 weeks is the anchor)
- **Results:** "Average client sees 247% ROI" (247% becomes the expectation)
- **Effort:** "Most consultants require a 6-month engagement. We deliver in 6 weeks."

## Serial Position Effect

People remember the first item (primacy) and last item (recency) in a list best.

**Copy implications:**

| List Type | Placement Strategy |
|---|---|
| Feature lists | Put your strongest feature first and your second-strongest last |
| Navigation | Most important page first (leftmost), second-most-important last (rightmost) |
| Pricing plans | Put the recommended plan in the center — visual primacy, but anchor with highest price first (left in LTR layouts) |
| Testimonials | Lead with the most specific/impressive, end with the most emotionally compelling |
| FAQ | First question = biggest objection. Last question = the close (soft CTA). |

## Processing Fluency

### Simpler = More Trustworthy

Research consistently shows that information presented in simpler, easier-to-process formats is perceived as more truthful and trustworthy (Reber & Schwarz, 1999).

**Copy implications:**
- Simple sentences are more persuasive than complex ones
- Concrete language ("We fixed 847 bugs") is more believable than abstract language ("We significantly improved quality")
- Rhyming increases perceived truthfulness ("If it doesn't fit, you must acquit" — McGlone & Tofighbakhsh, 2000)
- Familiar words are more trusted than unusual ones — "use" > "utilize"

### The Concreteness Advantage

Concrete words (things you can see, touch, count) are processed faster and remembered better than abstract words.

| Abstract (Weak) | Concrete (Strong) |
|---|---|
| "Improve operational efficiency" | "Cut report generation from 3 hours to 12 minutes" |
| "Enhance customer experience" | "Customers get answers in 4 minutes instead of 48 hours" |
| "Drive meaningful growth" | "Add $340K in annual recurring revenue" |

## Attention and Cognitive Load

### Banner Blindness

Users unconsciously ignore anything that looks like an advertisement — including legitimate content styled like ads (Benway & Lane, 1998). This includes:
- Anything in the right sidebar (often treated as ad space)
- Large colorful banners (processed as promotions)
- Pop-ups and overlays (dismissed before reading)

**Copy implication:** Critical messages should be in the content area, styled like content — not in banners, sidebars, or popups.

### Miller's Law (7 ± 2)

Working memory holds approximately 7 (± 2) items at once (George Miller, 1956).

**Copy implications:**
- Navigation items: 5-7 maximum
- Feature lists: chunk into groups of 3-5
- Steps in a process: 3-5 is ideal. If more, group into phases.
- Pricing plan features: highlight 5-7 key features, link to full comparison

### Cognitive Load Reduction

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Chunking** | Break long content into labeled sections |
| **Progressive disclosure** | Show summary first, detail on request |
| **Consistency** | Same patterns across pages reduce learning cost |
| **White space** | Visual breathing room reduces perceived complexity |

## Trust and Credibility

### Specificity = Credibility

Specific numbers are more believable than round numbers:

| Less Credible | More Credible |
|---|---|
| "Hundreds of clients" | "847 clients" |
| "About 50% improvement" | "47.3% improvement" |
| "Saves hours" | "Saves 3.2 hours per week" |

The precision signals that the number was actually measured, not estimated. (Schindler & Yalch, 2006)

### The Illusory Truth Effect

Statements that are repeated are perceived as more true (Hasher, Goldstein, & Toppino, 1977). Repeat your key message across multiple pages in different contexts — homepage hero, about page, testimonials, footer tagline.
