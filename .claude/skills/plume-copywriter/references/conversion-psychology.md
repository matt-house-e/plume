# Conversion Psychology — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Copywriting is persuasion. This file covers the psychological principles that make copy convert — from Cialdini's influence principles to pricing psychology and CTA optimization. Every principle includes specific copy applications, not just theory.

## Cialdini's 7 Principles Applied to Web Copy

Robert Cialdini's "Influence" (1984, updated 2021 with the 7th principle) provides the foundational persuasion framework. Here is how each principle manifests in website copy:

### 1. Reciprocity

Give something valuable before asking for anything.

| Copy Application | Example |
|---|---|
| Free tools / calculators | "Free ROI Calculator — See What Your Website Should Be Earning" |
| Valuable content without a gate | "Here's our complete guide — no email required" |
| Free audits / assessments | "Get a free 15-minute copy audit of your homepage" |
| Generous free tier | "Free forever for up to 3 projects" |

### 2. Social Proof

People follow the actions of others, especially similar others.

| Social Proof Type | Copy Pattern | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Numbers** | "Join 12,847 marketers who..." | Specific numbers > round numbers — precise figures signal measured data rather than estimates (Schindler & Yalch, 2006) |
| **Client logos** | "Trusted by [Logo Row]" | Use 5-8 recognizable logos. More ≠ better — diminishing returns after 8 |
| **Testimonials** | "[Specific result] — [Full name, role, company]" | Specific outcomes > generic praise. "Revenue up 34%" > "Great service!" |
| **Star ratings** | "4.9/5 from 2,300+ reviews on G2" | External review platforms are more credible than self-hosted reviews |
| **Case study snippets** | "How [Client] achieved [X] → Read the full story" | Combine metric + narrative for strongest proof |
| **"People like you"** | "Built for teams of 10-50 in B2B SaaS" | Specificity about who uses it signals fit |
| **Video testimonials** | 30-90 second clips with name/role overlay | Video testimonials increase conversion by ~80% over text (2024 data). Short-form video proof is now expected, not optional. |
| **UGC / social screenshots** | Screenshot of real tweet, review, or post | Unpolished, authentic content now outperforms curated testimonials — audiences are trained to distrust perfection |
| **Real-time proof** | "847 people viewed this today" / live user counts | Real-time social proof notifications boost conversions by ~15-25% (Fomo, ProveSource data) |

**2025 social proof hierarchy** (strongest → weakest):
1. Video testimonials with specific metrics
2. Named customer quotes with photo, role, and company
3. Third-party review platform ratings (G2, Trustpilot, Clutch)
4. UGC / social media screenshots
5. Client logos
6. Anonymous testimonials (weak — avoid when possible)

### 3. Authority

People defer to experts and established institutions.

| Authority Signal | Copy Pattern |
|---|---|
| Credentials | "Certified by [Authority]", "[X] years in [field]" |
| Media mentions | "As featured in [Publication]" — use recognizable logos |
| Expert endorsements | "[Quote] — [Expert Name, Title]" |
| Data/research | "According to our study of 1,200 websites..." — being the source of data is the strongest authority signal |
| Specificity | Precise numbers signal expertise. "We've optimized 847 landing pages" > "hundreds of landing pages" |

### 4. Liking

People buy from people (and brands) they like.

| Liking Trigger | Copy Pattern |
|---|---|
| Similarity | "We're a small team too — we know the struggle of doing everything yourself" |
| Warmth | Conversational tone, first-person stories, showing the humans behind the brand |
| Compliments | "You've already done the hardest part — building something people want" |
| Shared values | "We believe [value statement the audience agrees with]" |

### 5. Scarcity

People want what's limited. Use ethically — manufactured scarcity destroys trust.

| Ethical Scarcity | Example | Unethical (Avoid) |
|---|---|---|
| Genuine capacity limits | "We take on 5 clients per month" (if true) | Fake countdown timers that reset |
| Seasonal/launch pricing | "Launch price: $49 (regular: $99)" | "Only 3 left!" when supply is unlimited |
| Enrollment windows | "Doors open Jan 15-22" (if actually closed after) | Perpetual "limited time" offers |
| Beta/early access | "First 100 users get lifetime pricing" | Fake urgency on evergreen products |

### 6. Commitment & Consistency

People act consistently with prior commitments, especially public ones.

| Copy Application | Example |
|---|---|
| Micro-commitments before the big ask | Free trial → paid plan. Quiz → result → signup. |
| "Yes ladder" copy | Series of questions the reader says "yes" to: "Tired of X? Want Y? Ready for Z?" |
| Identity-based framing | "You're the kind of [marketer/founder/designer] who..." |
| Progress indicators | "You're 80% of the way there" — sunk cost motivates completion |

### 7. Unity (Cialdini's 7th principle, from "Pre-Suasion")

People are influenced by those they perceive as "one of us."

| Unity Trigger | Copy Pattern |
|---|---|
| Shared identity | "Built by developers, for developers" |
| In-group language | Using audience-specific terminology and references |
| Co-creation | "Shaped by feedback from 500 beta users" |
| Origin story | "We started this company because we had the same problem you do" |

## Objection Handling in Copy

### The 5 Universal Objections

Every product/service faces these. Address them before the reader voices them:

| Objection | Copy Counter-Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **"Too expensive"** | Reframe as ROI / cost of inaction | "The average client recovers their investment in 3.2 months" |
| **"I don't have time"** | Show speed / minimal effort required | "Setup takes 15 minutes. No learning curve." |
| **"It won't work for me"** | Specificity about who it works for + proof | "Designed for [their exact situation]. See how [similar company] did it." |
| **"I don't trust you"** | Stack social proof + guarantees | "30-day money-back guarantee. No questions. No hassle." |
| **"I can do it myself / later"** | Show the cost of waiting + DIY difficulty | "Every month you wait costs you ~$4,200 in missed conversions" |

### Where to Handle Objections

- **Inline:** Weave counter-arguments into body copy at natural tension points
- **FAQ section:** Address them directly as questions ("Is this really worth the price?")
- **Testimonials:** Choose testimonials that specifically counter top objections
- **Guarantee section:** A dedicated guarantee block removes risk

## Pricing Psychology in Copy

### Price Presentation Patterns

| Technique | How to Apply | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | Show the higher price first | "Normally $199/month — yours at $99/month" |
| **Charm pricing** | End in 7 or 9 for consumer, round numbers for premium | $97 (value), $100 (premium feel) — MIT study: prices ending in 9 outsell lower prices by 24% |
| **Per-unit reframing** | Break price into smaller units | "$3.29/day" instead of "$99/month" |
| **Value stacking** | List everything included before showing price | "Template library ($200 value) + weekly coaching ($500 value) + community access ($100 value) = $800 value → $149/month" |
| **Decoy pricing** | Offer 3 tiers where the middle tier is the target | The Economist study: adding a "print only" option at the same price as "print + digital" made 84% choose print + digital |
| **Annual vs. monthly** | Show annual savings as a percentage | "Save 20% with annual billing" — always show both prices, highlight annual |

### Pricing Page Copy Structure

1. **Headline:** Reframe from "what you pay" to "what you get" — "Choose the plan that fits your growth stage"
2. **Plan names:** Avoid generic (Basic/Pro/Enterprise). Use aspirational names or audience-based names (Starter/Growth/Scale, or Solo/Team/Business)
3. **Feature lists:** Lead with the features that differentiate plans, not the ones all plans share
4. **Recommended badge:** Flag one plan as "Most Popular" — social proof plus decision simplification
5. **FAQ below pricing:** Address price objections directly ("What happens if I outgrow my plan?")
6. **Guarantee:** Place guarantee copy directly below the CTA

## CTA Optimization

### CTA Copy Rules

| Rule | Rationale | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Use action verbs | Passive CTAs underperform | "Start Your Free Trial" not "Free Trial Available" |
| Include the benefit | Tell them what clicking gets them | "Get My Custom Report" not "Submit" |
| First-person can outperform | A/B tests have shown first-person CTAs outperforming second-person in some contexts — but results vary widely. Test both. | "Start My Free Trial" vs. "Start Your Free Trial" |
| Match the commitment level | The CTA should match how much you're asking | Free content: "Read Now" / Paid: "Start Free Trial" / High-ticket: "Book a Call" |
| Reduce perceived risk | Add risk-reversal near the CTA | "Start Free — No Credit Card Required" |

### CTA Text That Converts (by context)

| Context | Strong CTA | Weak CTA (Avoid) |
|---|---|---|
| Newsletter signup | "Get Weekly Tips" | "Subscribe" |
| Free trial | "Start My Free 14-Day Trial" | "Sign Up" |
| Demo request | "Book My Free Demo" | "Request Demo" |
| Contact form | "Send My Message" | "Submit" |
| E-commerce | "Add to Cart" / "Buy Now" | "Proceed" |
| Content download | "Download the Guide" | "Click Here" |
| Consultation | "Book My Free Strategy Call" | "Contact Us" |

### Surrounding CTA Copy

The text immediately above and below the CTA button matters:

- **Above:** One sentence restating the value prop or handling an objection
- **Below:** Risk reversal — "No credit card required", "Cancel anytime", "30-day money-back guarantee"
- **Urgency line** (when ethical): "Only 3 spots left for March" / "Price increases April 1"
