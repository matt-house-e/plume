# Inspiration Bank — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Well-structured websites to study and learn from, organized by site type. For each example: what makes its architecture effective, what structural patterns it demonstrates. Focus on information architecture and content strategy, not visual design (that's the design skill's territory).

## Portfolio / Personal Brand Sites

### Stripe Partners Directory
**URL:** https://stripe.com/partners
**What makes it effective:** Clean taxonomy (by region, capability, industry). Each partner page follows an identical template. Filters work intuitively. Demonstrates how to scale a portfolio-style section to hundreds of entries without chaos.
**Pattern:** Faceted filtering for large collections

### Sarah Drasner
**URL:** https://sarahdrasner.com/
**What makes it effective:** Minimalist structure — just the essential pages. Work is organized by type (writing, speaking, projects). The site lets the work speak for itself without architectural overhead.
**Pattern:** Lean portfolio for established personal brands

### Pentagram
**URL:** https://www.pentagram.com/
**What makes it effective:** Work is filterable by partner, discipline, and industry. Each case study follows a consistent narrative structure: challenge, approach, result. Navigation is minimal — the work IS the navigation.
**Pattern:** Filterable portfolio with consistent case study format

## Agency / Consultancy Sites

### Basecamp
**URL:** https://basecamp.com/
**What makes it effective:** Radically simple site structure. One product, one price, clear value proposition. The entire conversion funnel is visible from the homepage. Demonstrates how constraint creates clarity.
**Pattern:** Single-product clarity

### Ueno (now part of Twitter)
**URL:** https://ueno.co/ (archived)
**What makes it effective:** Case studies are the centerpiece. Each has a clear narrative arc. Services aren't listed separately — they're demonstrated through the work. About page focuses on culture and people, not corporate messaging.
**Pattern:** Case-study-driven agency site

### Instrument
**URL:** https://www.instrument.com/
**What makes it effective:** Work organized by capability and client. Clear about page with team, values, and approach. Clean content hierarchy — never more than 3 levels deep. News section is used strategically, not as a checkbox blog.
**Pattern:** Capability-organized agency with strategic content

## SaaS / Product Sites

### Linear
**URL:** https://linear.app/
**What makes it effective:** Homepage communicates the product's value in one scroll. Feature pages are deep without being overwhelming. Pricing is transparent with a clear comparison table. The entire site structure mirrors the product's philosophy of simplicity.
**Pattern:** Product-philosophy-consistent site architecture

### Notion
**URL:** https://www.notion.so/
**What makes it effective:** Use-case segmentation ("For Teams", "For Personal", "For Enterprise") lets different audiences find relevant content. Template gallery serves as both a feature showcase and SEO content. Resources section is a genuine learning hub.
**Pattern:** Multi-audience SaaS with use-case segmentation

### Vercel
**URL:** https://vercel.com/
**What makes it effective:** Developer-focused structure — documentation is a first-class citizen in the primary navigation. Feature pages use interactive demos instead of static descriptions. Pricing scales clearly from free to enterprise.
**Pattern:** Developer-first SaaS with docs-prominent navigation

### Ahrefs
**URL:** https://ahrefs.com/
**What makes it effective:** Content hub strategy — the blog, YouTube channel, and free tools all feed into the conversion funnel. Free tools (backlink checker, keyword generator) serve as micro-conversions. SEO-focused content demonstrates the product's own capabilities.
**Pattern:** Content-and-tools-driven SaaS conversion funnel

## E-commerce Sites

### Patagonia
**URL:** https://www.patagonia.com/
**What makes it effective:** Category structure balances product taxonomy (Jackets, Fleece, Baselayers) with use-case navigation (Climbing, Skiing, Trail Running). Environmental activism content is integrated, not siloed. Stories section connects products to purpose.
**Pattern:** Values-integrated e-commerce

### Glossier
**URL:** https://www.glossier.com/
**What makes it effective:** Minimal category structure — a curated selection, not a catalog. Each product page is a mini landing page with ingredients, routine placement, and social proof. The editorial blog (Into The Gloss) drives discovery.
**Pattern:** Curated e-commerce with editorial content

**URL:** (Historical reference)

## Content / Blog Sites

### Intercom Blog
**URL:** https://www.intercom.com/blog/
**What makes it effective:** Content organized by topic hubs (Customer Support, Product Management, Startups). Each hub has a pillar page linking to related articles. Clear content hierarchy: featured → recent → categorized. Newsletter signup is persistent but not intrusive.
**Pattern:** Topic hub content architecture

### Smashing Magazine
**URL:** https://www.smashingmagazine.com/
**What makes it effective:** Category structure matches how practitioners think (CSS, JavaScript, UX, Design). Search is prominent. Older content is still accessible and often updated. Membership model offers gated premium content alongside free articles.
**Pattern:** Practitioner-organized content site with membership tier

### Wait But Why
**URL:** https://waitbutwhy.com/
**What makes it effective:** Minimal structure — just posts, an archive, and an about page. No categories, no tags, no complex taxonomy. Demonstrates that for a single-author blog with a dedicated audience, architectural simplicity wins.
**Pattern:** Ultra-minimal blog architecture

**What makes it effective:** Impact storytelling is the center of the architecture. Every page leads to either "Donate" or "Learn More About Our Impact." The donate button is persistently visible. Project pages show real-world results with photos and data.
**Pattern:** Impact-driven non-profit architecture

**URL:** https://www.khanacademy.org/

## Documentation Sites

### Stripe Docs
**URL:** https://stripe.com/docs
**What makes it effective:** The gold standard for developer documentation. Progressive disclosure: Quick Start → Guides → API Reference. Code examples in multiple languages with tab switching. Search is fast and accurate. Sidebar navigation shows full structure without overwhelming.
**Pattern:** Progressive-disclosure developer documentation

### Tailwind CSS Docs
**URL:** https://tailwindcss.com/docs
**What makes it effective:** Every utility class has its own page with examples. Search is the primary navigation method. Left sidebar shows the full doc tree. Content is scannable with live examples. Demonstrates how to document a large API surface.

## Structural Patterns to Study

### The "Hub and Spoke" Pattern
**Used by:** Content sites, resource centers, knowledge bases
**Structure:** Central hub page (overview/directory) links to individual spoke pages (articles, guides, case studies). Each spoke links back to the hub and to related spokes.
**Example:** Intercom's blog topic hubs, HubSpot's resource center

### The "Funnel" Pattern
**Used by:** SaaS, e-commerce, lead generation
**Structure:** Each page narrows the focus and moves the visitor closer to conversion. Awareness → Interest → Evaluation → Action.
**Example:** Basecamp's homepage → features → pricing → signup

### The "Library" Pattern
**Used by:** Documentation, educational sites, content-heavy resources
**Structure:** Large collection organized by taxonomy with strong search. Progressive disclosure from overview to detail.
**Example:** Stripe Docs, MDN Web Docs, Khan Academy

### The "Showcase" Pattern
**Used by:** Portfolios, agencies, case study-driven businesses
**Structure:** Work is the primary content. Navigation routes to filterable gallery. Each piece expands into a detailed case study.
**Example:** Pentagram, Instrument

### The "Storefront" Pattern
**Used by:** E-commerce, marketplace, product catalog
**Structure:** Category hierarchy with filtering. Product pages are the conversion point. Supporting content (about, blog, FAQ) surrounds the catalog.
**Example:** Patagonia, Glossier

## How to Study a Site for Structure

When analyzing an inspiration site:

1. **Ignore the visual design.** Focus on what pages exist and how they connect.
2. **Map the navigation.** Write down every nav item and its children.
3. **Count pages.** Estimate total page count from the sitemap and footer.
4. **Trace a journey.** Start from the homepage and try to reach the conversion point. Count clicks.
5. **Check mobile.** How does the navigation adapt? What's prioritized?
6. **Read the footer.** Footers reveal the full site structure that the primary nav hides.
7. **Try to get lost.** Can you reach a dead end? An orphan page? A confusing label?
