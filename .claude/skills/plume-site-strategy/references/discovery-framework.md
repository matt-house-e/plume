# Discovery Framework — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Discovery is the foundation of website strategy. Bad discovery produces bad architecture. This reference provides interview questions scaled by client maturity, input checklists, techniques for extracting strategy from clients who haven't articulated it, and facilitated workshop formats for complex engagements.

## Extracting Strategy from Inarticulate Clients

When a client says "I just need a website," use these techniques to surface latent strategy:

### The "Magic Wand" Technique
"If this website could do ONE thing perfectly — attract the exact right person, communicate the exact right message, and get them to take the exact right action — what would that look like?"

### The "Last Client" Technique
"Tell me about your last great client. How did they find you? What did they need? What made them choose you over alternatives?"

This surfaces: ideal customer profile, acquisition channel, value proposition, differentiators.

### The "Competitor Walk" Technique
"Let's look at [competitor]'s website together. What do you like? What would you do differently? What's missing?"

This surfaces: design preferences, positioning instincts, competitive awareness — things the client can't articulate in abstract but can express through comparison.

### The "If-Then" Technique
"If someone who's perfect for your business lands on your homepage, then what? What do you want them to think, feel, and do in the first 10 seconds?"

### The "Elevator Pitch" Technique
"If you met your ideal client in an elevator and had 30 seconds, what would you say?"

### The "Day in the Life" Technique
"Walk me through a typical day for your ideal customer. What frustration are they dealing with that leads them to search for someone like you?"

### The "Kill the Feature" Technique
When a client wants everything: "If you could only have 5 pages on this website, which 5? Now which 3? Now which 1?" Forces prioritization through constraint.

## Facilitated Workshop Format (Agency/Enterprise)

For complex engagements with multiple stakeholders, run a structured workshop instead of individual interviews.

### Workshop Agenda (90-120 minutes)

**Part 1: Alignment (20 min)**
- Introductions and roles
- Current state: "What's working and what's not?" (round-robin, 2 min each)
- Success definition: "What does a successful website look like 6 months after launch?"

**Part 2: Audience & Goals (30 min)**
- Audience identification exercise: Each stakeholder writes their top 2 audience segments on cards → group and merge → rank by business value
- Goal prioritization: Each stakeholder writes their top 3 website goals → group → force-rank using dot voting (each person gets 3 dots)

**Part 3: Content & Structure (30 min)**
- Card sorting exercise: List all potential pages on cards → each stakeholder groups them into categories → compare groupings → identify consensus and conflicts
- Navigation sketching: Each stakeholder sketches their ideal primary nav (5-7 items) → compare → identify consensus

**Part 4: Decision & Next Steps (20 min)**
- Review decisions made
- Identify unresolved conflicts → assign owner + deadline
- Agree on approval process going forward (who has final say)

### Workshop Rules
- One stakeholder is designated the "decision maker" — they break ties
- All conflicts are documented, not suppressed — record dissenting views
- "Phase 2" is a valid resolution for non-critical disagreements
- The strategist facilitates but doesn't defer to the loudest voice — data and user needs arbitrate

## Common Discovery Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Skipping discovery entirely | Architecture based on assumptions, not evidence | Always do at least a lightweight interview |
| Asking only about what clients WANT | Missing what visitors NEED | Balance "what do you want?" with "what do your customers look for?" |
| Taking the brief at face value | Real goals are often unstated or different from the brief | Probe beneath stated goals with "why" questions |
| Not asking about competitors | Missing industry patterns and expectations | Always identify 3-5 competitors, even if the client resists |
| One-sided discovery | Getting the business perspective without the customer perspective | Ask for analytics, customer feedback, support tickets, reviews |
| Not documenting decisions | Same questions revisited in Phase 3, 4, 5 | Write the Discovery Summary immediately, get it signed off |
| Over-discovering | 3-hour interview for a 5-page portfolio site | Scale discovery to client maturity — 20 min for solo, 2 hours for enterprise |
