# Positioning Framework — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Positioning is the strategic foundation that all marketing builds on. It answers: "What is this, who is it for, and why should they care?" Every downstream decision — messaging, channels, pricing, content, design — derives from positioning. Getting it right makes everything easier; getting it wrong makes everything else a waste.

## April Dunford's 5-Component Framework

From *Obviously Awesome* (2019). Work components in order — each step informs the next.

### Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** What would customers use if this business did not exist?

Go beyond direct competitors. Alternatives include:
- Direct competitors (similar products/services)
- Indirect competitors (different approach, same problem)
- Manual processes ("doing it themselves with spreadsheets")
- Hiring someone (a freelancer, an employee, a consultant)
- Doing nothing (living with the problem)

**How to identify:** Ask best customers: "Before you found us, what were you doing to solve this problem?" Cluster answers into 2-5 primary alternatives.

### Component 2: Unique Attributes

**Question:** What do you have that the alternatives do not?

List every differentiating feature, capability, or characteristic:
- Product features or technical capabilities
- Delivery model (speed, format, access)
- Proprietary methodology or process
- Specific expertise or credentials
- Business model advantages (pricing, terms)
- Support quality or customer experience
- Network effects or community

**Critical rule:** Only list what can be objectively proven. "Better quality" is not unique unless you can point to a specific, measurable attribute that creates the quality difference.

### Component 3: Value (and Proof)

**Question:** What do those unique attributes enable for customers?

Translate attributes into customer value using the chain: Feature → Benefit → Value.

| Attribute | Benefit | Value |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| AI-powered matching algorithm | Find relevant candidates 3x faster | Fill positions before competitors poach talent |
| 20 years in healthcare consulting | Deep understanding of regulatory landscape | Avoid compliance mistakes that cost $100K+ |
| Same-day turnaround | Get deliverables when you need them | Never miss a deadline with a client |

Cluster related values into 2-3 "value themes" that become messaging pillars.

**Proof required:** Every value claim needs evidence — metrics, case studies, testimonials, credentials. Unproven claims are noise.

### Component 4: Target Customer Characteristics

**Question:** Who cares the most about this value?

Define the actionable characteristics that make someone a best-fit buyer:
- Not "small businesses" but "B2B SaaS companies with 10-50 employees"
- Not "people who want to get fit" but "busy professionals over 35 who have tried gyms and quit"
- Not "anyone who needs a website" but "solo consultants making $200K+ who want premium positioning"

**Dunford's principle:** Narrow ruthlessly. Positioning for everyone means positioning for no one. You can expand later — start specific.

### Component 5: Market Category

**Question:** What frame of reference makes this value obvious?

The category triggers expectations about features, pricing, and competitors. Choosing the right one transforms perception.

**Example:** A database company repositioned from "enterprise database" (competing against Oracle) to "embeddable database for IoT devices" (competing against SQLite). Same product, different category, different competitive set.

## Three Positioning Styles

### Big Fish, Small Pond (Recommended for Most Startups)

Dominate a specific subsegment of an existing category. Leverages category awareness while avoiding direct competition with leaders.

**When to use:** The market has an established category but no one owns the subsegment you serve best.

**Example:** Instead of "CRM software" → "CRM for investment banks." Then expand: investment banks → retail banking → all financial services.

**Advantages:** Lower marketing cost (existing category understanding), clear differentiation, premium pricing possible, easier to become the obvious choice.

### Head-to-Head

Compete to win an existing category outright.

**When to use:** You have the market leader's product quality AND significantly more resources, or the market has no clear winner.

**Risk:** Requires substantial resources. Not recommended for bootstrapped companies or early-stage startups.

### Create a New Category

Establish an entirely new market category.

**When to use:** Only if the offering genuinely cannot be understood through any existing frame.

**Risk:** Requires enormous marketing investment to educate the market. Most companies should not attempt this. Per Dunford: "Most successful companies were initially positioned in underserved subsegments of existing categories."

**Warning signs you should NOT create a category:**
- An existing subsegment can be claimed with less effort
- Budget does not support 12-24 months of category education
- The "new category" is really just a rebrand of something customers already understand

## Positioning Statement Templates

### April Dunford's Positioning Canvas (Preferred)

Do not compress positioning into a single sentence. Document all 5 components:

```markdown
## Positioning Canvas

**Competitive Alternatives:** [What customers would use instead]
**Unique Attributes:** [What we have that alternatives don't]
**Value:** [What those attributes enable — with proof]
**Target Customers:** [Who cares most — with specificity]
**Market Category:** [The frame that makes our value obvious]
**Positioning Style:** [Big Fish / Head-to-Head / New Category]
**(Optional) Relevant Trend:** [Tailwind that makes this timely]
```

### Geoffrey Moore Template (for external communication)

> For **(target customer)** who **(statement of need)**, the **(product/business)** is a **(category)** that **(key benefit)**. Unlike **(primary alternative)**, we **(primary differentiator)**.

### Service Business Template

> I help **(specific audience)** achieve **(specific outcome)** through **(specific method/approach)**.

The more specific each variable, the stronger the positioning.

## Positioning for Service Businesses

Service businesses position on expertise and approach, not product features:

| Specialization Axis | Example |
|---------------------|---------|
| **Industry vertical** | "Copywriter for health tech brands" |
| **Problem type** | "I fix broken onboarding flows" |
| **Methodology** | "Data-driven brand strategy using customer interviews" |
| **Audience segment** | "Executive coaching for first-time CTOs" |
| **Outcome** | "Double your qualified leads in 90 days" |

**The specialist vs generalist dynamic:** Most successful service providers are generalists who market as specialists. Broad capabilities, narrow positioning. Specialize now, expand later once trust and authority are established.

## Testing Positioning

### The 30-Second Stranger Test

Show the homepage or pitch to someone unfamiliar with the business for 30 seconds. Then ask:
1. What does this company do?
2. Who is it for?
3. Why would someone choose this over alternatives?

If they cannot answer clearly, positioning needs work. Research shows users abandon pages within 10-20 seconds without clear value.

### Five Clarity Questions

Ask 3-5 team members or stakeholders to answer independently:
1. Who is your target customer?
2. What problem are you solving?
3. What is your solution?
4. What makes you different?
5. Why should they care now?

**Failure signal:** Wildly different answers across respondents.

### Customer Interview Validation

Focus on the natural language customers use:
- How do they describe the problem?
- What words do they use to describe your value?
- What alternatives did they consider?

Slack shifted from "a tool for teams" to "be less busy" based on actual user language.

### Data-Driven Testing

- Test headline variations on landing pages — track bounce and conversion rates
- A/B test positioning angles in ad copy — track click-through performance
- Monitor sales cycle length — shorter cycles indicate clearer positioning

## Repositioning: When and How

### Warning Signs Current Positioning Is Broken

1. Current customers love you but new prospects do not understand what you sell
2. Long sales cycles and low close rates
3. Constant price pressure (indicating lack of differentiation)
4. High post-purchase churn from misaligned expectations
5. Team gives inconsistent answers about what the company does

### Repositioning Options

1. **Strengthen:** Reinforce original positioning with sharper language and better proof
2. **Narrow:** Claim a more specific subsegment (Big Fish, Small Pond)
3. **Reframe:** Change the market category to make value more obvious
4. **Overhaul:** Fundamental positioning change (product pivot, new market)

### Cadence

Revisit positioning every 6 months. Formally reposition only when major signals warrant it. Most products undergo multiple repositionings over their lifecycle.
