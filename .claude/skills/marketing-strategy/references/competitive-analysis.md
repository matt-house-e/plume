# Competitive Analysis — Reference Guide

## Purpose

Competitive analysis for marketing strategy is about understanding how competitors position themselves and where opportunities exist — not building feature comparison matrices. The output should change how the business allocates budget and handles objections, not produce a checklist.

## Types of Competitors

| Type | Definition | Why It Matters |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Direct** | Same product/service, same audience | Immediate revenue threat; must differentiate clearly |
| **Indirect** | Different approach, same problem | Shape buyer expectations; "good enough" alternatives |
| **Aspirational** | Where you want to be; benchmarks | Set the bar for excellence; study for patterns |
| **Adjacent** | Neighboring markets, overlapping customers | Future threats; watch for expansion signals |
| **Do Nothing** | Customer lives with the problem | Often the biggest competitor; overcome inertia |

**Practical guidance:** Focus on 3-5 competitors total. A focused analysis is far more valuable than a broad, shallow one.

## The 2x2 Positioning Map

### How to Create One

**Step 1: Choose axes based on buyer decision-making.** Axes must reflect how buyers evaluate and disqualify vendors, not how you see yourself.

**Effective axis pairs:**
- Speed vs depth
- Simplicity vs control
- Breadth vs specialization
- Affordability vs capability
- Self-serve vs high-touch
- Niche vs broad market
- Customizable vs standardized

**Axis selection rules:**
- Must matter for customer choice
- Must show real spread across competitors
- Must not be highly correlated with each other
- "A good axis creates tension. 'Innovation' is useless because everyone claims it."

**Step 2: Gather evidence.** Five signals to build from:
1. Homepage messaging — what vendors emphasize first
2. Pricing architecture — reveals business model and target segment
3. Feature packaging — shows what is bundled and prioritized
4. External buyer language — reviews, comparisons, community discussions
5. Change velocity — how quickly the company evolves positioning

**Step 3: Plot competitors honestly.** Use customer perception data when available, not internal wishful thinking.

**Step 4: Identify your quadrant.** Strong positioning means choosing a quadrant to defend, not claiming to serve everyone.

### Common Mistakes
- **The "Fake Matrix":** Placing yourself in the flattering top-right corner. This creates positioning disconnected from reality.
- **Company-centric axes** instead of buyer concerns
- **Static thinking** — the map should show directional movement over time

### Advanced: Add a Third Dimension
Use bubble size to represent annual revenue, market share, or community size.

## What to Analyze About Each Competitor

### Messaging and Positioning
- Homepage headline and value proposition
- Who they say they serve (target audience signals)
- Brand voice and tone
- How positioning has shifted over time (use Wayback Machine at archive.org)

### Pricing
- Pricing tiers and packaging structure
- Free tier or trial strategy
- Pricing model (per seat, usage-based, flat rate, project-based)

### Marketing Channels and Content
- Primary acquisition channels (organic, paid, referral, partnerships)
- Content strategy (blog topics, frequency, depth)
- Social media presence and engagement
- Email marketing approach
- Community building efforts

### SEO Footprint
- Target keywords and ranking positions
- Top-performing content
- Backlink profile and domain authority
- Content gaps to exploit

### Customer Sentiment
- Review patterns on G2, Capterra, Trustpilot
- Common complaints and praise themes
- Support quality indicators

### Hiring Patterns
- Job postings reveal future priorities before announcements
- New roles signal strategic bets
- Leadership changes indicate strategy shifts

## Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas

### How to Create One

**Horizontal axis:** List the factors the industry competes on (price, quality, speed, service, features, support, customization, brand prestige, etc.).

**Vertical axis:** Rating scale (low to high).

**Plot value curves:** Connect dots showing each competitor's offering level across all factors. Your curve should look fundamentally different.

### The ERRC Grid

| Action | Question | Purpose |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors should you drop entirely? | Remove costs and complexity |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should you reduce below industry standard? | Free resources for differentiation |
| **Raise** | Which factors should you raise above industry standard? | Create clear superiority |
| **Create** | Which factors should you offer that no one else does? | True differentiation |

**Example — Casella Wines ([yellow tail]):** Eliminated wine complexity, aging prestige, and wine range. Reduced vineyard prestige. Raised price above budget wines, retail involvement. Created easy drinking, easy selection, fun positioning. Created a new market of non-wine-drinkers.

## White Space Identification

### Five Categories of White Space

1. **Functional gaps** — existing products do not fully solve consumer problems
2. **Emotional needs** — current brands fail to address authentically
3. **Usage occasions** — no products designed for specific contexts
4. **Demographic segments** — underserved by current offerings
5. **Channel opportunities** — products not available through preferred methods

### Visualization Technique

Create a matrix: consumer segments across the top, product attributes down the left side. Mark cells where competitors have strong offerings. Empty cells are potential white spaces.

### What Makes White Space Valuable

The best opportunities:
- Address genuine, persistent pain points
- Align with the business's unique capabilities
- Have reasonable barriers to competitor entry
- Can be validated with potential customers before launch

## Avoiding the Feature Comparison Trap

Feature-to-feature comparison is fundamentally flawed because customers do not want features — they want to get their job done.

**The Zune cautionary tale:** Microsoft's Zune had more features than the iPod. It captured zero meaningful market share. Feature parity is not a strategy.

**What to compare instead:**
- Positioning and value proposition
- Customer outcomes and results
- Experience and service quality
- Business model and pricing innovation

**Jobs-to-Be-Done approach:** Identify the customer's job. Measure how well competitors help complete that job. Position where competitors fail to help customers get the job done fast or accurately enough.

## Ongoing Competitive Monitoring (20 Minutes/Week)

### One-Time Setup (30 minutes)
1. Google Alerts for each competitor name + key terms
2. Subscribe to competitor newsletters and blogs
3. Follow competitors on LinkedIn and Twitter/X
4. Bookmark key pages: pricing, product, changelog, careers
5. Create a shared tracking document

### Weekly Routine (20 minutes)
- Skim competitor updates
- Check for pricing or homepage changes
- Note new content themes or messaging shifts
- Log findings in tracking doc

### Monthly Review (30 minutes)
- Review competitor reviews on G2/Capterra
- Check careers pages for hiring signals
- Scan for funding or partnership announcements
- Update positioning map if needed

**Key principle:** 90% attention on your users, 10% on competitors. Not every competitor launch merits a reaction.
