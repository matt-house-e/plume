# Strategic Thinking — Reference Guide

## Purpose

This reference covers the cognitive framework behind marketing strategy — the principles that separate strategic thinking from tactical guesswork. These are the foundations that do not change regardless of channel or trend.

## Four First Principles of Marketing Strategy

From Palmatier & Sridhar (AMS Review):

1. **All customers differ.** Segmentation is not optional. One-size-fits-all messaging fails.
2. **All customers change.** Markets shift; needs evolve. Strategy must account for change.
3. **All competitors react.** Any successful tactic will be copied. Sustainable advantage comes from positioning, not tactics.
4. **All resources are limited.** You cannot do everything. Focus is the core strategic decision.

## The Correct Sequence

**Positioning before promotion.** The positioning statement is the tune; the marketing mix is the orchestration. If a marketing activity does not reinforce positioning, it creates confusion.

**Audience before message.** You cannot craft a compelling message without deeply understanding who you are talking to, what they care about, and what alternatives they are considering.

**Message before medium.** The channel should be chosen based on where the defined audience congregates, not based on what is trending.

**Strategy before tactics.** Strategy answers "why" and "where." Tactics answer "how." A strategy should remain stable for 12-24 months. Tactics change quarterly.

## Strategy vs Tactics

| Dimension | Strategy | Tactics |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Question** | Why and where? | How and what? |
| **Time horizon** | 12-24 months | Weekly to quarterly |
| **Stability** | Changes rarely | Changes frequently |
| **Example** | "Position as the premium option for mid-market SaaS" | "Post 3x/week on LinkedIn about founder lessons" |
| **Owner** | CEO/CMO/founder | Marketing team/executor |
| **Measurement** | Market position, brand perception, unit economics | Impressions, clicks, conversions, CAC |

### The Strategy Gap

84% of CMOs report high levels of strategic dysfunction (Gartner, March 2025) — primarily because they are consumed by tactical execution rather than strategic leadership.

Emily Kramer calls the symptom "Random Acts of Marketing (RAM)" — check-the-box marketing without regard for what will actually work for a specific startup.

### When Strategy Changes

**Does not warrant strategy change:**
- A blog post flopped
- A competitor launched a feature
- A social media trend emerged
- Q1 results were below target

**Does warrant strategy change:**
- Product pivot or major feature shift
- ICP no longer matches best customers
- Market shift (regulation, technology, category disruption)
- PMF signals weakening (rising churn, declining organic growth)
- Competitor fundamentally changes the category

## Common Founder Marketing Mistakes

### 1. No Positioning (Trying to Appeal to Everyone)
42% of startup failures are attributed to "no market need" (CB Insights). Often rooted in weak marketing validation — the company never defined who it was for.

### 2. Copying Competitor Tactics Without Strategy
Copying Notion's template strategy without having Notion's product mechanics is a recipe for waste. Tactics only work in the context of the strategy they were designed for.

### 3. Premature Paid Advertising
Running paid ads before organic traction proves the funnel works. Without product-market fit signals, paid media burns budget without sustainable growth. Linear grew to $1.25B valuation spending only $35K total on paid marketing.

### 4. Spreading Across Too Many Channels
The #1 channel strategy mistake. 9% of startups fail due to founder burnout, often from spreading thin across 5+ channels at 20% effort each.

### 5. Vanity Metrics Obsession
Tracking followers, impressions, and page views instead of CAC, LTV, conversion rates, and revenue per channel. Creates false confidence while the business is not growing.

### 6. No Measurement Framework
Without dashboards and regular data reviews, teams cannot determine which efforts drive growth and which are waste.

### 7. Building Before Validating
Skipping customer discovery. As Rob Fitzpatrick teaches in *The Mom Test*: never assume you understand user needs without talking to them first.

### 8. Confusing Being Busy with Being Effective
Posting daily on 4 platforms, attending every event, writing weekly blog posts — all without connecting these activities to a strategic goal or measuring their impact.

## The Marketing Strategy Document

### What It Should Contain

A real marketing strategy brief is 5-15 pages. Not a 50-page deck.

1. **Objective / Purpose** — What business outcome are we driving?
2. **Positioning** — How we are different and why it matters
3. **Target Audience (ICP)** — Specific segments with depth
4. **Core Messaging** — Value propositions, proof points, narrative
5. **Channel Strategy** — Which 1-3 channels to invest in and why
6. **Goals & Metrics** — How to measure success (leading + lagging)
7. **Budget / Resources** — Cost and time allocation
8. **Timeline / Milestones** — Key dates and phases
9. **Team / Roles** — Who owns what

### Emily Kramer's GACCS Brief (Lightweight Alternative)

- **G**oals — What you are trying to accomplish
- **A**udience — Who you are targeting
- **C**hannels — How you will distribute
- **C**reative — The actual content/fuel
- **S**takeholders — Who needs involvement

### Strategy Review Cadence

| Frequency | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Weekly** | Execute and track tactical performance |
| **Monthly** | Analyze channel performance, identify what works |
| **Quarterly** | Full tactical review — adjust channels, messaging, budget |
| **Annually** | Strategic refresh — reassess positioning, audience, direction |

The strategy itself (positioning, ICP, core messaging) should not change every quarter — only the tactical execution plan should.

## Mental Models for Marketing Strategy

### The Flywheel (HubSpot)

Replace the funnel with a flywheel: Attract → Engage → Delight → (customers become advocates who attract more customers). Energy compounds. Friction between stages slows the wheel.

### Jobs-to-Be-Done

Customers do not buy products; they "hire" them to do a job. Marketing should describe the job being done, not the product's features. Format: "When [situation], I want to [job], so I can [outcome]."

### The Minimum Viable Marketing Stack

Before doing anything else:
1. A clear homepage that explains what you do, for whom, and why
2. An email capture mechanism
3. One piece of valuable content per week
4. One active social channel where the ICP spends time
5. Basic analytics (GA4 + UTMs + self-reported attribution)

Everything else is an add-on.

## The 2025-2026 Marketing Landscape

### Key Shifts

- **AI content flood:** The internet is saturated with AI-generated content. Differentiation now requires first-hand experience, genuine opinion, specific stories, and a voice that could not belong to anyone else.
- **Privacy and attribution erosion:** Cookie deprecation, iOS privacy changes, and regulation reduce tracking ability. Self-reported attribution becomes essential.
- **Dark funnel dominance:** 70-80% of B2B evaluation happens in channels invisible to analytics. Strategy must account for unmeasurable influence.
- **Founder brand as moat:** In an era of AI-generated everything, authentic founder voices become the last defensible marketing advantage.
- **CAC inflation:** CAC jumped 40-60% across channels between 2023-2025. Organic and community channels become more valuable as paid gets more expensive.
- **Community-led growth:** Companies like Notion (280K+ subreddit), Figma (designer communities), and Linear (16K+ Slack) prove that community is a durable growth channel.

### What Remains Evergreen

- Positioning determines everything downstream
- Depth beats breadth in channel strategy
- Email is still the highest-ROI channel
- SEO compounds over time and cannot be bought
- Word of mouth is the most credible form of marketing
- The best marketing is a great product
