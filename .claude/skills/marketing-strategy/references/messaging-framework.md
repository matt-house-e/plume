# Messaging Framework — Reference Guide

## Purpose

The messaging framework defines what to say before deciding how to say it. Clear messaging is 2x as influential as design in converting customers (Wynter research). This reference covers narrative structure, messaging hierarchy, value propositions, proof architecture, objection handling, and awareness calibration.

## StoryBrand SB7 Framework (Donald Miller)

The central principle: **the customer is the hero; the brand is the guide.**

Narrative structure: A CHARACTER who wants something encounters a PROBLEM. At the peak of their despair, a GUIDE steps into their lives, gives them a PLAN, and CALLS THEM TO ACTION. That action helps them avoid FAILURE and ends in SUCCESS.

| Part | What It Covers | Key Detail |
|------|---------------|------------|
| **1. Character** | What does the customer want? | ONE primary desire. A third-grader should understand it. |
| **2. Problem** | Three layers of obstacle | **External:** the tangible blocker. **Internal:** the emotional frustration. **Philosophical:** the broader injustice. |
| **3. Guide** | Your brand enters as mentor | Two requirements: **Empathy** (show you understand) + **Authority** (demonstrate credibility). |
| **4. Plan** | 3-4 clear steps | **Pre-purchase:** (Schedule call → Diagnose → Build plan). **Post-purchase:** (Onboard → Support → Succeed). |
| **5. Call to Action** | Direct + Transitional | **Primary CTA:** direct commitment. **Transitional CTA:** low-stakes for hesitant prospects. |
| **6. Failure** | Stakes of inaction | Lost opportunities, competitive disadvantage, continued frustration. Creates urgency without manipulation. |
| **7. Success** | The transformed future | Paint a vivid picture of life after solving the problem. |

### BrandScript Template

```markdown
## BrandScript

**Character:** [Customer] wants [desire].
**External Problem:** [Tangible obstacle]
**Internal Problem:** [How it makes them feel]
**Philosophical Problem:** [Why this is unjust — "People shouldn't have to..."]
**Guide:** We understand [empathy statement]. We have [authority proof].
**Plan:** Step 1: [action]. Step 2: [action]. Step 3: [action].
**Call to Action:** [Primary CTA]. Or start with [transitional CTA].
**Failure:** Without this, [negative consequences].
**Success:** With this, [transformed future].
```

## Messaging Hierarchy

Structure messages at three levels of depth for different contexts:

### Tier 1: One-Liner / Tagline (10 words or fewer)

A promise, not a description. Passes the "billboard test" — graspable at 60 mph.

**Formats that work:**
- "[End result] without [pain point]"
- "[Audience] + [outcome]"
- "[Verb] [what] [how]"

**Examples:** "Be less busy" (Slack). "Design together" (Figma). "Move fast, stay aligned" (Linear).

### Tier 2: Elevator Pitch (2-3 sentences, ~20-30 seconds)

Expand the tagline with specificity. Must answer: Who are you? What do you do? How do you do it? Who do you serve?

**Formula:** Hook + Problem + Solution + Proof + CTA.

### Tier 3: Full Value Narrative (1 paragraph, 75-150 words)

The complete story with context, differentiation, proof, and emotional resonance. Used in About pages, investor decks, sales conversations.

## Value Proposition Frameworks

### The "Only We" Test

Complete this sentence: "Only we [verb] [what] for [whom] by [how]."

If a competitor could credibly say the same thing, it is not specific enough. Replace abstractions with specifics:
- BAD: "We help businesses grow." (Anyone could say this.)
- GOOD: "We help B2B SaaS companies under 50 employees double qualified leads in 90 days using SEO content their competitors haven't thought to create."

### Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas

**Customer Profile (start here):**
- **Jobs-to-be-Done:** Functional tasks, social tasks, emotional tasks
- **Pains:** Negative experiences, risks, obstacles
- **Gains:** Positive outcomes and benefits they expect

**Value Map:**
- **Products & Services:** Most important components of the offering
- **Pain Relievers:** How the offering alleviates specific pains
- **Gain Creators:** How the offering delivers positive outcomes

Fit is achieved when Pain Relievers and Gain Creators directly address the most important Pains and Gains.

### Five Stress-Test Questions (LSA Global)

1. Are we the ONLY company that does what we do the way we do it?
2. Are we so much cheaper or have such a better pricing model to truly set us apart?
3. Do we clearly do it better, easier, more personalized, more innovative?
4. Do we do it measurably faster or with more responsiveness?
5. Do we provide unmatched breadth/depth that cannot be found elsewhere?

If the answer to ALL five is "no," the value proposition needs work.

## Proof Points and Evidence

### Types of Proof

| Type | Best For | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Metrics / Data** | Logical decision-makers, enterprise | "35% increase in conversion rate" |
| **Customer Testimonials** | Trust building, social proof | Direct quote with name and role |
| **Case Studies** | Complex sales, B2B | Full before/after narrative with results |
| **Credentials** | Regulated industries, trust signals | Certifications, partnerships, awards |
| **Customer Logos** | B2B, enterprise trust | Recognizable brand names |
| **User Statistics** | Scale and momentum | "10,000+ companies trust us" |
| **Expert Endorsements** | Niche markets, thought leadership | Industry authority recommendation |
| **Guarantees** | Risk reduction | Money-back, SLA commitments |
| **Before/After** | Tangible outcomes | Visual or data transformation evidence |

### Organizing Proof by Message Pillar

For each supporting message (3-5 pillars), map 2-3 proof points:

```markdown
**Pillar 1: [Claim]**
- Proof: [Metric or case study]
- Proof: [Testimonial]
- Proof: [Credential]

**Pillar 2: [Claim]**
...
```

## Objection Handling Map

### The Four Categories

1. **Price:** "It costs too much" / "We don't have budget"
2. **Product fit:** "Does it really do what we need?"
3. **Trust:** "How do we know this works?"
4. **Timing:** "Now isn't the right time" / "Too complex to switch"

### How to Identify Top Objections

- Analyze sales calls for recurring concerns
- Interview lost deals: why did they not buy?
- Survey customers: what almost stopped you?
- Track FAQ patterns and support tickets

### Counter-Message Techniques

| Technique | Pattern | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Feel-Felt-Found** | Acknowledge, normalize, resolve | "Other clients felt the same way. What they found was..." |
| **Boomerang** | Transform objection into reason to buy | "The reason it costs more is precisely because it includes X, which saves Y." |
| **Proactive placement** | Address before prospects raise it | FAQ section, testimonial from initial skeptic, pricing page with ROI calc |

**Best practice:** Embed objection handling throughout marketing content before prospects raise concerns — in FAQ sections, testimonials from initial skeptics, and pricing pages with ROI data.

## Point of View / Contrarian Narrative

### Dave Gerhardt's Framework

The single most defensible differentiator is a unique point of view on the market. AI and competitors can replicate features; they cannot replicate perspective.

**How to develop a POV:**
1. **Identify the villain:** What is the broken status quo? "The way everyone does [X] is broken because [Y]."
2. **Take a stand:** Have a strong opinion about how things should be. "We believe [Z]."
3. **Be willing to alienate:** A real POV means saying no to certain audiences.
4. **Back it up:** POV without proof is just bluster. Connect it to evidence and results.

### POV Template

```markdown
## Our Point of View

**The conventional wisdom:** [What most people in the market believe]
**Why it's wrong:** [The evidence or insight that challenges it]
**What we believe instead:** [Your contrarian position]
**What this means for customers:** [How this belief translates to better outcomes]
```

## Eugene Schwartz's 5 Awareness Levels

Determines headline sophistication and copy structure for every piece of marketing.

| Level | Customer Knows | Headline Approach | Copy Length |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|------------|
| **1. Unaware** | Nothing — no problem awareness | Stories, provocative questions, indirect | Long-form narrative, educational |
| **2. Problem-Aware** | Has the problem, no solution awareness | Lead with the problem, agitate pain | Problem validation → solution bridge |
| **3. Solution-Aware** | Solutions exist, does not know YOUR product | Lead with desired result, differentiate | Testimonials, case studies, comparisons |
| **4. Product-Aware** | Knows you, not yet convinced | Lead with unique advantage, handle objections | Offers, risk reversal, comparisons |
| **5. Most Aware** | Everything — ready to buy | Direct: show the price, CTA | Short copy, price, urgency |

**Key principle:** Headline sophistication is inversely correlated with awareness. Unaware audiences need the most sophisticated, indirect headlines. Most-aware audiences just need the price and a button.

**Practical application:** Determine where the majority of the target audience falls. Homepage typically targets Solution-Aware to Product-Aware. Blog content targets Problem-Aware to Solution-Aware. Retargeting ads target Product-Aware to Most Aware.
