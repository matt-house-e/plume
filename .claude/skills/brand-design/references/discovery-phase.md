# Discovery & Positioning — Reference Guide

## Purpose

The discovery phase exists to prevent designing in a vacuum. Every visual decision downstream should trace back to a strategic insight uncovered here. Skip this phase and the brand becomes decoration; do it well and the brand becomes inevitable.

> "Strategy is about making choices, trade-offs; it's about deliberately choosing to be different." — Michael Porter

## Discovery Framework

### 1. Stakeholder Input Gathering

Collect answers to these questions (from the user, or infer from project context):

**Identity Questions:**
- What do you/your brand do in one sentence?
- What makes you different from others doing similar work?
- If the brand were a person, how would they dress? How would they speak?
- What three words should someone use to describe you after visiting your site?
- What existing brands do you admire (even outside your industry)? Why?

**Audience Questions:**
- Who is your primary audience? (Role, seniority, technical level)
- What problem are they trying to solve when they find you?
- What is their emotional state when they arrive? (Curious? Urgent? Skeptical?)
- Where else do they spend time online?
- What would make them trust you within 5 seconds?

**Aspiration Questions:**
- Where is the brand heading in 2-3 years?
- What would wild success look like?
- What should the brand NEVER become?

### 2. Competitive Audit Methodology

**Process:**
1. Identify 5-8 peers/competitors (direct and aspirational)
2. For each, analyze using WebFetch to retrieve HTML/CSS, and extract:
   - Color values (from CSS custom properties, stylesheets)
   - Font families (from CSS declarations and font CDN links)
   - Layout structure (from HTML elements and CSS grid/flex)
   - Content strategy (from headings, hero text, CTAs)
3. Ask the user to share screenshots of competitor sites they admire — visual judgment is best done collaboratively
4. For each competitor, document:
   - Color palette, typography approach, layout pattern
   - Motion approach (ask user: "does this site use animation?")
   - Overall mood (3 adjectives)
5. Map all competitors on a 2x2 positioning matrix

**Recommended 2x2 axes** (choose the most relevant pair):
- Formal ←→ Casual × Technical ←→ Accessible
- Minimal ←→ Maximal × Traditional ←→ Experimental
- Corporate ←→ Personal × Conservative ←→ Bold

**Output:** A visual positioning map showing where competitors cluster and where the white space is. The brand should aim for open territory.

### 3. Brand Attribute Exercise

**The "This, Not That" Method:**

Generate 3-5 attribute pairs that define the brand's personality:

| We are... | We are NOT... |
|-----------|---------------|
| Precise | Rigid |
| Warm | Casual |
| Forward-thinking | Trendy |
| Grounded | Boring |
| Craft-oriented | Precious |

**The Spectrum Method:**

Place the brand on each spectrum:

```
Playful ━━━━━━━━●━━━━ Serious
Minimal ━━●━━━━━━━━━━ Maximal
Abstract ━━━━━━━━━●━━ Literal
Loud ━━━━━━━━━━━●━━━ Quiet
Classic ━━━━━●━━━━━━━ Modern
```

These spectrums become the creative brief for all visual decisions.

### 4. Brand Brief Document Template

```markdown
# Brand Brief

## Purpose
[One sentence: what the brand exists to do]

## Audience
- **Primary:** [Who they are, what they need]
- **Secondary:** [If applicable]
- **Emotional context:** [How they feel when they arrive]

## Positioning
- **Category:** [What space you operate in]
- **Differentiation:** [What makes you distinct]
- **Competitors:** [3-5 names with 1-line positioning notes]
- **White space:** [Where you're going that others aren't]

## Brand Attributes
[3-5 "This, Not That" pairs]

## Brand Spectrums
[5-7 spectrum positions]

## Anti-Attributes
[What the brand explicitly rejects]

## Reference Brands
[2-3 brands admired for specific qualities, with "why"]
```

## Competitive Audit: What to Actually Look At

When studying competitor sites, capture these specific observations:

### Visual Observations
| Aspect | What to Note |
|--------|-------------|
| **First impression** | 3 adjectives that describe the gut feeling within 2 seconds |
| **Color** | Primary color, accent color, background treatment (light/dark/neutral) |
| **Typography** | Headline typeface, body typeface, overall type density |
| **Layout** | Hero pattern, grid usage, whitespace density (generous/moderate/dense) |
| **Imagery** | Photography style, illustration presence, icon style |
| **Motion** | Animation level (none/subtle/moderate/heavy), scroll behavior |
| **Personality** | Does it feel corporate/personal/playful/serious/technical/creative? |

### Strategic Observations
| Aspect | What to Note |
|--------|-------------|
| **Positioning** | How do they describe what they do? (first sentence on the site) |
| **Differentiator** | What do they claim makes them unique? |
| **Proof** | How do they demonstrate credibility? (metrics, logos, testimonials, case studies) |
| **CTA** | What is their primary call to action? How prominent? |
| **Audience signal** | Who does the site seem designed for? (technical vs non-technical, enterprise vs startup) |

### The Positioning Map

After auditing 5-8 competitors, plot them on a 2×2 matrix:

```
                     Formal/Corporate
                          │
                          │
         Enterprise  ─────┼───── Startup/Personal
                          │
                          │
                     Casual/Creative
```

Find the quadrant with the most white space. That's the positioning opportunity.

## Brand Personality: The 12 Archetypes

Carl Jung's brand archetypes can accelerate personality definition. Which archetype is the brand closest to?

| Archetype | Motivation | Voice | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|-------|----------|
| **The Creator** | Innovation, self-expression | Original, imaginative | Apple, Adobe |
| **The Sage** | Knowledge, truth | Informed, analytical | Google, McKinsey |
| **The Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | Bold, independent | Patagonia, NASA |
| **The Hero** | Mastery, achievement | Confident, direct | Nike, FedEx |
| **The Magician** | Transformation | Visionary, charismatic | Tesla, Disney |
| **The Rebel** | Revolution, disruption | Provocative, bold | Harley-Davidson, Virgin |
| **The Everyperson** | Belonging, connection | Honest, relatable | IKEA, Target |
| **The Caregiver** | Service, protection | Warm, reassuring | Johnson & Johnson |
| **The Ruler** | Control, stability | Authoritative, premium | Mercedes, Rolex |
| **The Jester** | Joy, humor | Fun, irreverent | Old Spice, Mailchimp |
| **The Lover** | Intimacy, beauty | Passionate, sensual | Chanel, Godiva |
| **The Innocent** | Simplicity, optimism | Pure, hopeful | Coca-Cola, Dove |

**For a personal brand:** You likely won't be a pure archetype, but identifying the 1-2 closest helps guide tone, imagery, and visual direction.

## Sources & Further Reading

- **Michael Johnson, "Branding: In Five and a Half Steps"** — Best published end-to-end brand design process. The "investigate" phase maps directly to discovery.
- **Marty Neumeier, "The Brand Gap"** — Foundational strategic thinking on differentiation and brand as gut feeling.
- **Collins (agency)** — Their "Creative Platform" concept shows how to distill strategy into a generative creative direction. See case studies at wearecollins.com.
- **IDEO Human-Centered Design Toolkit** — The "Hear" phase methodology applies directly to discovery interviews.
- **Brand New (UnderConsideration)** — underconsideration.com/brandnew — Ongoing analysis of real rebrand projects; excellent for competitive research and understanding industry norms.
- **"The Hero and the Outlaw" (Mark & Pearson)** — The canonical reference on brand archetypes.
- **Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas** — strategyzer.com — Framework for mapping audience needs to brand offerings.
